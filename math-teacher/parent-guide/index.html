‚è±Ô∏è Session Structure (10-15 Minutes)

Short, focused sessions are more effective than long ones. Here's a proven structure:

The 10-15 Minute Session

Time Activity Purpose
2 min Warm-up: Quick review question from previous lesson Activate prior knowledge
5-8 min Main activity: Concrete ‚Üí Pictorial ‚Üí Abstract New learning or practice
3-5 min Reflection: "What did you learn?" / "Teach me what you did" Consolidate understanding
üí° Pro Tip

End sessions while your child is still engaged, not when they're frustrated. It's better to do 10 focused minutes than 30 struggling minutes.

‚ùì Questions to Ask (Instead of Giving Answers)

Your job is not to explain - it's to help your child discover. Use these questions to guide their thinking:

When Starting a Problem

What do you notice about this problem?
What information do we have?
What is the problem asking us to find?
Does this remind you of anything we've done before?

When They're Stuck

Can you draw a picture of this?
What would this look like with real objects?
What do you already know that might help?
What if we tried a simpler version first?

When They Get an Answer

How do you know that's right?
Can you explain how you got that?
Does that answer make sense? How can you check?
Could you solve it a different way?

For Deeper Understanding

Why does that work?
What would happen if we changed this number?
Can you teach me how to do this?
Where else might you use this in real life?
üéØ The Goal

Your child should be doing 80% of the talking. If you find yourself explaining, stop and ask a question instead.

üå± Responding to Mistakes

How you respond to mistakes shapes whether your child sees math as threatening or as a puzzle to solve. Mistakes are not problems to fix‚Äîthey're windows into your child's thinking.

‚úÖ Do Say

  • "Interesting! Tell me more about how you got that."
  • "I see what you're thinking. Let's look at this together."
  • "That's a really common thing to think. Let me show you something..."
  • "You're on the right track! Let's check this part."
  • "I love that you tried. What could we do differently?"

‚ùå Don't Say

  • "No, that's wrong."
  • "We just went over this!"
  • "Think harder."
  • "That doesn't make sense."
  • "You should know this by now."

Sample Dialogue: When They Make a Mistake

Child: 1/3 + 1/3 = 2/6

Parent: Interesting! Tell me how you got 2/6.

Child: I added the tops and added the bottoms.

Parent: I see your thinking! Let's check that with our fraction tiles. Can you show me 1/3 + 1/3 with the pieces?

Child: [Uses tiles, discovers it's 2/3, not 2/6]

Parent: What do you notice? Why do you think the denominator didn't change?

‚ö†Ô∏è Important

Never show frustration, even if you feel it. Take a breath. If you're getting frustrated, it's time to take a break. Say: "Let's come back to this later with fresh eyes."

üîÑ The CPA Approach

Every lesson in this curriculum follows the Concrete ‚Üí Pictorial ‚Üí Abstract (CPA) progression. This is how Singapore Math builds deep understanding.

üß±

CONCRETE

Physical manipulation with real objects

  • Fraction tiles/circles
  • Paper folding
  • Play-doh/clay
  • Food (pizza, chocolate bars)
  • Walking a number line
‚úèÔ∏è

PICTORIAL

Drawing representations

  • Bar models
  • Number lines
  • Circles/rectangles
  • Drawings of situations
  • Diagrams
üî¢

ABSTRACT

Symbols and equations

  • Fraction notation (3/4)
  • Equations
  • Algorithms
  • Mental math
  • Word problems
üéØ The Rule

Never skip straight to abstract. If your child can do the procedure but can't explain why it works, go back to concrete or pictorial. The goal is understanding, not just correct answers.

üö© Red Flags to Watch For

These responses indicate conceptual gaps, not careless errors:

üö© "I just memorized it"

What it means: They're doing procedures without understanding

What to do: Ask "Can you show me with the tiles?" or "Can you draw it?"

üö© Adding denominators (1/3 + 1/3 = 2/6)

What it means: They don't understand that the denominator names the SIZE of pieces

What to do: Go back to concrete with fraction tiles. Ask: "Do the pieces change size when we combine them?"

üö© "Larger denominator means larger fraction"

What it means: They think 1/8 > 1/4 because 8 > 4

What to do: Use pizza analogy: "Would you rather have 1 slice when the pizza is cut into 4 pieces or 8 pieces?"

üö© "Fractions are always less than 1"

What it means: They haven't grasped improper fractions

What to do: Build 5/4 with circles. Ask: "How many fourths do you have? Is that more or less than 1 whole?"

üö© Can't explain WHY an answer is correct

What it means: They may be pattern-matching or guessing

What to do: Always ask "How do you know?" Even for correct answers!

üö© Consistent correct answers with no understanding

What it means: They've memorized the procedure without meaning

What to do: Give them a variation they haven't seen. If they can't transfer, go back to concrete.

üß± When to Go Back to Concrete

Go back to hands-on activities whenever you see:

  • Confusion or frustration
  • Correct procedures without explanation
  • Any of the red flags above
  • A new concept being introduced
  • Questions that start with "Why..."

Quick Concrete Activities

Paper Folding

Fold paper to show halves, fourths, eighths. Great for seeing equivalence (fold a half in half to see 2/4 = 1/2).

Fraction Tiles

Colored tiles that snap together. Stack them to compare, combine for addition, find equivalents.

Food Division

Chocolate bars, pizza, sandwiches. Real sharing problems with real consequences!

Masking Tape Number Line

Put tape on the floor, mark 0 and 1, then have your child physically walk to fraction positions.

Parent: I can see you're getting stuck. Let's get out the tiles and build this!

Child: But I know how to do it with numbers...

Parent: Great! But I want to understand it better. Can you teach me using the tiles?
