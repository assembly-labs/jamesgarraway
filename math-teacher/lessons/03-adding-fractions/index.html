Lesson 3: Adding Fractions

Combining same-sized pieces

üíª ONLINE LESSON - Do this on the computer with a parent or guardian!
‚Üê Lesson 2: Improper Fractions Lesson 4: Subtracting Fractions ‚Üí

üö® Watch Out!

Big mistake alert: Some people add BOTH numbers (like 1/4 + 2/4 = 3/8). Nope! The bottom number tells you the SIZE of the pieces - and that doesn't change when you combine!

üéØ What You'll Learn

Adding fractions is like combining catches in a game. Here's what you'll figure out:

  • When the bottom numbers match, you're counting same-sized pieces
  • Add the top numbers (how many pieces) but keep the bottom the same (piece size doesn't change!)
  • It answers: "How many pieces do we have now?"

üß† The Big Idea: Adding Fractions = Counting More Pieces!

The Simple Explanation:

When you add fractions with the same bottom number, you're just counting more pieces of the SAME size. Think of it like this: if you have 2 quarters and someone gives you 1 more quarter, you now have 3 quarters. You don't suddenly have "thirds" - the pieces are still quarters!

So 2/4 + 1/4 = 3/4. Add the top numbers (2 + 1 = 3), keep the bottom the same (still fourths). The bottom number tells you the SIZE of the pieces, and that doesn't change just because you have more of them!

üèà The Football Way to Think About It:

Imagine a football field split into 10 equal sections (each section is 10 yards). Justin Jefferson catches a pass and runs 3/10 of the field (30 yards). On the next play, he catches another and runs 2/10 more (20 yards). How far did he go total?

You don't change the field into 20 sections! The field is still split into 10 parts. He just covered MORE of those parts: 3/10 + 2/10 = 5/10 of the field (50 yards). Add the catches, keep the field the same size!

‚õèÔ∏è The Minecraft Way to Think About It:

You're building a wall that's 8 blocks wide. You place 3/8 of the blocks (3 blocks), then your friend places 4/8 more blocks (4 blocks). How much of the wall is done?

The wall is still 8 blocks wide - that didn't change! You just placed more of those 8 blocks: 3/8 + 4/8 = 7/8 of the wall is complete. One more block and you're done!

‚öΩ The Soccer Way to Think About It:

A soccer game has 2 halves. If you played 1/2 of the first game and 1/2 of the second game, you played 1/2 + 1/2 = 2/2 = 1 whole game's worth of time! The halves are still halves - you just played more of them.

Concrete Activities

Hands-on exploration

Counting Pizza Slices

Use fraction circles or draw two paper plate "pizzas" cut into 8 equal slices each.

You ate 2 slices from the first pizza, and 3 slices from the second pizza. Both pizzas were cut into eighths. How many eighths did you eat in total?

Let your child physically count the pieces: 2 eighths + 3 eighths

Are all the slices the same size? (Yes - they're all eighths!) So when we count them together, how many eighths do we have? (5 eighths!)
Key Insight: "We're counting pieces of the SAME SIZE. 2 eighths + 3 eighths = 5 eighths. The denominator (8) tells us the size of each piece - that doesn't change!"

The "Same Size" Rule

Set out fraction pieces in fourths. Give your child 1 fourth and then 2 fourths separately.

You have 1 fourth here, and 2 fourths here. If you push them all together, how many fourths do you have?

Let them combine the pieces and count: 3 fourths total.

Did the pieces change size when you pushed them together? (No!) So what stays the same in our answer? (The denominator - they're still fourths!)

Now try with other denominators:

  • 3/10 + 2/10 (using tenths)
  • 1/6 + 4/6 (using sixths)
  • 2/5 + 2/5 (using fifths)

Why DON'T We Add Denominators?

This is the most important question! Let's explore it.

Some kids think 1/4 + 2/4 = 3/8. Let's test that with real pieces. Take out 1 fourth. Now add 2 more fourths. Do you have 3 eighths, or 3 fourths?

Let them see that the pieces are still the SAME SIZE (fourths), so we have 3 fourths, not 3 eighths.

üèà Football Connection: Justin Jefferson's Big Catches

Justin Jefferson catches two passes, each gaining a fraction of the field (think of the 100-yard field divided into 10 sections of 10 yards each).

  • First catch: Justin gained 3/10 of the field (30 yards)
  • Second catch: Another great catch for 2/10 of the field (20 yards)
  • Total yards: 3/10 + 2/10 = 5/10 of the field (50 yards)
Each section is still the same size (10 yards, or 1/10 of the field). We're just counting how many of those sections Justin covered: 3 sections + 2 sections = 5 sections!
Try this: Cooper DeJean intercepts a pass and returns it 4/10 of the field. Then the Eagles offense gains another 3/10. What fraction of the field have the Eagles covered? (Answer: 4/10 + 3/10 = 7/10)

Key insight: The size of the field sections doesn't change - we're just counting how many we've covered!

Abstract: The Rule

Connecting to symbols and procedures

The Addition Rule (Same Denominators)

2 5
+
1 5
=
3 5

The thinking process:

  1. Check the denominators: Are they the same? (Both are 5 - good!)
  2. Think about what that means: We're counting fifths + fifths
  3. Add the numerators: 2 fifths + 1 fifth = 3 fifths
  4. Keep the denominator: They're still fifths! So denominator stays 5
‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Understanding

The procedure "add the tops, keep the bottom" only makes sense when you understand WHY. Always ask yourself: "What size pieces am I counting?"

Practice Problems

Solve these, but for EACH one, explain your thinking out loud:

  1. 3/8 + 2/8 = ?
  2. 1/6 + 4/6 = ?
  3. 5/10 + 3/10 = ?
  4. 2/4 + 3/4 = ? (This will be improper - that's okay!)
For each problem, have your child explain: "I have ___ [size pieces] plus ___ more [same size pieces], which gives me ___ [size pieces] altogether."

üí≠ Understanding Check

Before finishing, make sure you can answer these:

Why DON'T we add denominators?

(Because the denominator tells us the SIZE of the pieces, and that doesn't change when we combine them. We're still counting the same-sized pieces!)

What ARE we adding?

(The numerators - the number of pieces. We're counting how many pieces we have altogether.)

Can the answer be bigger than 1?

(Yes! If we add enough pieces, we can have more than one whole. For example, 3/4 + 2/4 = 5/4 = 1 1/4)
