Lesson 2: Fractions Bigger Than 1!

Wait... fractions can be MORE than 1 whole? Let's find out!

üíª ONLINE LESSON - Do this on the computer with a parent or guardian!

üö® Heads Up!

A lot of people think fractions have to be smaller than 1. Surprise - they don't! Fractions can be bigger than 1 whole. This lesson is all about understanding how that works.

üéØ What You'll Learn

By the end of this lesson, you'll know:

  • The bottom number (denominator) tells you the SIZE of each piece
  • The top number (numerator) tells you HOW MANY pieces you have
  • You can have MORE pieces than fit in one whole - that's called an improper fraction
  • You can write the same amount two different ways (like 5/4 and 1 1/4 - same thing!)

üß† The Big Idea: Fractions Can Be MORE Than 1!

The Simple Explanation:

Most people think fractions are always less than 1, like 1/2 or 3/4. But surprise - fractions can be BIGGER than 1 whole! Here's the secret: the bottom number (denominator) just tells you what SIZE your pieces are. The top number (numerator) tells you how MANY pieces you have. And you can have as many pieces as you want!

If each piece is 1/4 (a quarter), and you have 5 of them, that's 5/4. That's MORE than one whole! We call this an "improper fraction." You can also write it as 1 1/4 (one and one-fourth) - same exact amount, just written differently.

üèà The Football Way to Think About It:

A football game has 4 quarters, right? So 1 quarter = 1/4 of a game. But what if the Eagles play a game that goes into overtime? They might play 5 quarters! That's 5/4 of a regular game - more than one whole game!

Or think about Jalen Hurts throwing touchdown passes. If he throws 7 touchdowns over a weekend (in 2 games), and each game has 4 quarters, he played 8 quarters total. But 7 touchdowns in those 8 quarters means... wait, that's a LOT of scoring! The point is: you can count MORE quarters than fit in one game.

‚õèÔ∏è The Minecraft Way to Think About It:

In Minecraft, a stack of items is 64. So 1 item = 1/64 of a stack. But what if you're mining diamonds and you collect 80 diamonds? That's 80/64 of a stack - more than one full stack! You'd have 1 full stack of 64, plus 16 extra. That's the same as 1 and 16/64 (or 1 and 1/4 of a stack). Same amount, two ways to write it!

ü•ã The Jiu-Jitsu Way to Think About It:

In jiu-jitsu class, let's say each round of sparring is 5 minutes. If you roll for 12 minutes straight, that's 12/5 of a round - more than 2 whole rounds! You could say you did 2 full rounds plus 2/5 of another round. Same training time, just counted differently.

Concrete Activities

Hands-on exploration

The "Too Many Pieces" Challenge

Give your child fraction circles cut into fourths (4 pieces each). Give them TWO complete circles.

Here's your challenge: Count out 5 fourths. Put 5 quarter pieces in front of you. You've got this!

Let them work. They'll figure out they need pieces from BOTH circles!

Whoa, what do you notice? How many complete circles did you make? And how many extra pieces are left over?

5/4 looks like this:

+

1 whole circle + 1 extra quarter = 1 1/4

Key Insight: "5/4 means 5 pieces where each piece is 1/4 of a circle. You have MORE than one whole circle's worth!"

What Do the Numbers Mean?

Build several improper fractions together and discuss each part:

Let's build 7/4. First, what does the 4 tell us?

Guide them to understand:

  • Denominator (4): "Each whole is cut into 4 pieces. Each piece is called a FOURTH."
  • Numerator (7): "We have 7 of those fourth-sized pieces."
If someone has 7 fourths, do they have more or less than 1 whole? How do you know?

Now try: 6/4, 9/4, 10/4 - build each one!

Walk the Number Line

Create a number line on the floor with masking tape. Mark 0, 1, and 2.

This is our fraction number line. We're going to WALK to different fractions. I want you to walk to 5/4. Where do you think that is?

Key points to make:

  • 4/4 = 1 (standing at 1)
  • 5/4 is ONE MORE fourth past 1
  • 8/4 = 2 (standing at 2)
