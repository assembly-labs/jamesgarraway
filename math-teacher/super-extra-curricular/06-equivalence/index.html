Lesson 6: Equivalent Fractions

Creating fractions that represent the same amount

üíª ONLINE LESSON - Do this on the computer with a parent or guardian!

üéØ What You'll Learn

Some fractions are secretly twins - same amount, different names! Here's what you'll figure out:

  • Equivalent fractions = different names for the SAME amount
  • How to make equivalent fractions (multiply top AND bottom by the same number)
  • A cool math trick: multiplying by 2/2 or 3/3 is like multiplying by 1!
  • Why this matters for adding fractions later on

üß† The Big Idea: Fraction Twins - Same Amount, Different Names!

The Simple Explanation:

Equivalent fractions are like twins - they look different but represent the EXACT same amount. 1/2, 2/4, 3/6, 4/8, 5/10 - these are ALL the same! Half a pizza is half a pizza, no matter how many slices you cut it into.

Here's the trick: if you multiply (or divide) BOTH the top and bottom by the same number, you get an equivalent fraction. Multiply 1/2 by 2/2 and you get 2/4. Why does this work? Because 2/2 = 1, and multiplying by 1 doesn't change anything!

üèà The Football Way to Think About It:

Jordan Love and the Packers are at the 50-yard line. That's 50/100 of the field (50 out of 100 yards). But we could also call it 5/10 of the field, or 1/2 of the field. All three fractions describe the EXACT same spot on the field - midfield!

It's like having different ways to give directions to the same place. "Midfield," "the 50," "halfway" - all the same spot, just different names.

‚õèÔ∏è The Minecraft Way to Think About It:

You have half an inventory full of cobblestone. That's 18/36 slots (since an inventory has 36 slots). But you could also call it 1/2, or 9/18, or 2/4 of your inventory. All those fractions mean the same thing: HALF your inventory is full!

Doesn't matter if you count by ones, by twos, or by nines - half is half!

ü•ã The Jiu-Jitsu Way to Think About It:

You spend half your class doing technique drills. Whether you say "1/2 of class," "2/4 of class," or "30/60 minutes" (if class is an hour) - it's all the same amount of time! The fraction looks different, but you're still drilling techniques for the same number of minutes.

üí° Why Does This Matter?

Equivalent fractions are SUPER important later when you need to add fractions with different bottom numbers. For example, to add 1/2 + 1/4, you'll change 1/2 into 2/4 (its twin!), then add: 2/4 + 1/4 = 3/4. The twins help fractions work together!

Concrete Activities

Hands-on exploration

The Matching Game

Use fraction circles. Take 1/2 of one circle.

You have 1/2. Can you find other fraction pieces that are the exact same size as this half? Try different pieces!

Let them discover: 1/2 = 2/4 = 3/6 = 4/8 (all the same amount!)

Are all these fractions the same size? (Yes!) So they're all different NAMES for the same amount!
Key Insight: "1/2, 2/4, 3/6, and 4/8 all represent the exact same amount. They're equivalent fractions - different names, same amount!"

Splitting Pieces

Give your child 1 third piece.

You have 1/3. If we split each third in half, how many pieces would we have? What would we call those new pieces?

Let them work it out: If we split each of the 3 thirds in half, we'd have 6 sixths total. And 1/3 becomes 2/6!

Finding the Pattern

Let's look at the pattern when we create equivalents:

Look at 1/2 = 2/4. What happened to the numerator? (1 became 2 - we multiplied by 2) What happened to the denominator? (2 became 4 - we multiplied by 2 too!)
The Pattern!

To make equivalent fractions, we multiply (or divide) BOTH the numerator and denominator by the SAME number!

üèà Football Connection: Packers at Midfield!

Jordan Love leads the Packers to the 50-yard line. Is being at the 50-yard line (5/10 of the field) the same as being at midfield (1/2 of the field)?

  • 5/10 of the field = 50 yards
  • 1/2 of the field = 50 yards
  • These are equivalent! 5/10 = 1/2
The announcer might say "The Packers are at midfield" or "They've covered half the field" or "They're at the 50" - all the same position!
Think about it: If the Eagles have covered 2/4 of the field and the Ravens have covered 4/8 of the field, are they at the same position? (Answer: Yes! 2/4 = 4/8 = 1/2)

Key insight: Equivalent fractions give us flexibility in how we describe amounts!

Creating Equivalent Fractions

The Rule:

To create an equivalent fraction, multiply both numerator and denominator by the same number.

This works because you're really multiplying by n/n, which equals 1, and multiplying by 1 doesn't change the value!
