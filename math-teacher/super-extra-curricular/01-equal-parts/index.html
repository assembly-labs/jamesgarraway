Lesson 1: Equal Parts

What makes parts TRULY equal?

üéØ What You'll Learn

Fractions only work when something is split into truly equal parts. By the end of this lesson, you'll be able to spot equal parts AND create them yourself!

üß† The Big Idea: What Are Equal Parts?

The Simple Explanation:

When you cut something into pieces, those pieces are "equal" only if they're the EXACT same size. Not "kinda the same" or "close enough" - they have to be EXACTLY the same. If you cut a pizza into 4 slices but one slice is huge and another is tiny, those aren't equal parts! Fractions only work when every piece is the same size as the others.

üèà The Football Way to Think About It:

Picture a football field. It's 100 yards long, and it's marked every 10 yards with those white lines. Each section between the lines is EXACTLY 10 yards - not 9 yards here and 11 yards there. That's why refs can make fair calls! If you're at the 50-yard line, you're EXACTLY in the middle. The field only works because every 10-yard section is truly equal.

Or think about dividing your flag football team into groups. If you have 8 players and need 2 equal groups, each group needs EXACTLY 4 players. You can't put 5 in one group and 3 in another and call them "equal" - that wouldn't be fair!

‚õèÔ∏è The Minecraft Way to Think About It:

In Minecraft, every block is the EXACT same size - 1 cubic meter. When you build a 4-block wide bridge, each block takes up exactly 1/4 of the width. That's why building works perfectly in Minecraft! Imagine if blocks were random sizes - your builds would be a total mess. Equal parts make everything work.

Concrete Activities

Hands-on exploration with real objects

Paper Folding: Making Halves

Take a sheet of paper and fold it in half.

Before you fold, here's a challenge: What should be true about the two parts when you fold it in half? Make a prediction!

Let your child fold the paper. Then ask them to open it up.

How can you PROVE these are truly equal halves? Here's a pro tip: Try stacking one part on top of the other!

Now try folding the same paper a different way (hamburger vs hotdog fold). Ask:

Are both of these still halves? How do you know?
Key Insight: There's more than one way to divide something into equal parts! Both are halves because both pieces are the same size, even though they're different shapes.

Paper Folding: Making Fourths

Now challenge your child to fold a NEW piece of paper into four equal parts.

Can you figure out how to fold this paper so you have 4 equal parts? There's more than one way to do it!

Encourage them to try multiple approaches:

  • Fold in half, then in half again (makes 4 rectangles)
  • Fold diagonally both ways (makes 4 triangles)
  • Fold in half horizontally, then vertically (makes 4 squares)
Are all these fourths? How can you prove it?

Have them cut out the pieces and stack them to verify they're equal.

The Unfair Sharing Game

Take a piece of play-doh and make it into a rectangle (like a brownie).

I'm going to share this brownie between us... but wait, I'm going to do it UNFAIRLY on purpose. Watch carefully and catch me being sneaky!

Deliberately cut it into unequal pieces and take the bigger one. Ham it up!

Hey, is this fair? What's wrong with how I shared it? How would YOU fix it to make it truly fair?

Let your child fix it by making truly equal parts.

Here's the big idea: When we say 1/2, we mean 1 of 2 EQUAL parts. If the parts aren't equal, it's not really 1/2! That's why equal parts matter so much.

Equal Parts Detective

Make several shapes with play-doh and divide them different ways - some equal, some NOT equal.

Time to be a detective! Some of these shapes are divided into truly equal parts... and some are just PRETENDING. Can you spot the fakes?

For each shape, ask:

  • "Are these equal parts? How do you know?"
  • "How could you prove it?" (Take apart and compare)
  • "If this isn't equal, how could you fix it?"

Draw Your Own Equal Parts

Using blank paper, draw rectangles and divide them:

  1. Draw a rectangle and divide it into 2 equal parts (TWO different ways)
  2. Draw a rectangle and divide it into 4 equal parts (TWO different ways)
  3. Draw a rectangle and divide it into 3 equal parts
After each one, ask: "How do you know these parts are equal?"
Common Challenge: Thirds are harder than halves or fourths! That's okay. The struggle is part of learning. Ask: "What makes thirds trickier?"

üèà Football Connection: Eagles Practice Drills

Imagine you're the Eagles' coach and you need to divide the practice field into equal sections for Jalen Hurts, Cooper DeJean, and the rest of the team.

  • If you have 2 groups (offense and defense), you need halves - 2 equal sections
  • If you have 4 position groups, you need fourths - 4 equal sections
  • The football field is 100 yards. If you divide it into 4 equal parts, how long is each part? (25 yards!)
Think like a coach: If the Packers' practice field is split unfairly, some players get more room than others. Jordan Love wouldn't have as much space to throw as he needs!

Key insight: The parts MUST be equal or some players would have more room than others - not fair!

Connecting to symbols

Now that you understand equal parts, let's connect to fraction notation:

=
1 4

1/4 means: 1 part out of 4 EQUAL parts

Vocabulary Check
  • Denominator (bottom number): Tells how many EQUAL parts the whole is divided into - it's the "name" of the fraction (fourths, eighths, etc.)
  • Numerator (top number): Tells how many parts we're talking about
Why is it SO important that the parts are equal? What would happen if 1/4 didn't mean equal fourths?
